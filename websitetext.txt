<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">A common high-grade metamorphic rock</div>
<table class="infobox"><caption class="infobox-title">Gneiss</caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" class="infobox-subheader" style="background-color: #cddeff"><a href="/wiki/Metamorphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Metamorphic">Metamorphic rock</a></td></tr><tr><td colspan="2" class="infobox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Gneiss.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Gneiss.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gneiss.jpg/220px-Gneiss.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gneiss.jpg/330px-Gneiss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gneiss.jpg/440px-Gneiss.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="361" /></a><div class="infobox-caption">Sample of gneiss exhibiting "gneissic banding".</div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Gneiss</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a common and widely distributed type of <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rock</a>. Gneiss is formed by high-temperature and high-pressure <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> processes acting on formations composed of <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>. Orthogneiss is gneiss derived from <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rock</a> (such as granite). Paragneiss is gneiss derived from <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> (such as sandstone). Gneiss forms at higher temperatures and pressures than <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a>. Gneiss nearly always shows a banded texture characterized by alternating darker and lighter colored bands and without a distinct foliation.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>gneiss</i> has been used in English since at least 1757. It is <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">borrowed</a> from the German word <i>Gneis</i>, formerly also spelled <i>Gneiss</i>, which is probably derived from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_High_German" title="Middle High German">Middle High German</a> noun <i lang="gmh" title="Middle High German (ca. 1050-1500)-language text">gneist</i> "spark" (so called because the rock glitters).<sup id="cite_ref-etymonline_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gneiss is formed from <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Igneous" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous">igneous</a> rock exposed to temperatures greater than 320&#160;°C and relatively high pressure.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gneissic rocks are usually medium- to coarse-<a href="/wiki/Foliation_(geology)" title="Foliation (geology)">foliated</a>; they are largely <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(geology)" title="Recrystallization (geology)">recrystallized</a> but do not carry large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">micas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorite_group" title="Chlorite group">chlorite</a> or other platy <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. Gneisses that are metamorphosed igneous rocks or their equivalent are termed <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> gneisses, <a href="/wiki/Diorite" title="Diorite">diorite</a> gneisses, and so forth. Gneiss rocks may also be named after a characteristic component such as <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnet</a> gneiss, <a href="/wiki/Biotite" title="Biotite">biotite</a> gneiss, <a href="/wiki/Albite" title="Albite">albite</a> gneiss, and so forth.  <i>Orthogneiss</i> designates a gneiss derived from an <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rock</a>, and <i>paragneiss</i> is one from a <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg/220px-Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg/330px-Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg/440px-Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2485" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orthogneiss_Geopark.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orthogneiss from the Czech Republic</div></div></div>
<p><i>Gneissose</i> rocks have properties similar to gneiss.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gneissic_banding">Gneissic banding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gneissic banding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gneiss appears to be striped in bands like parallel lines in shape, called gneissic banding.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshak_2009_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshak_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The banding is developed under high temperature and pressure conditions.
</p><p>The minerals are arranged into layers that appear as bands in cross section.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshak_2009_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshak_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The appearance of layers, called 'compositional banding', occurs because the layers, or bands, are of different composition. The darker bands have relatively more <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> minerals (those containing more <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>). The lighter bands contain relatively more <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic</a> minerals (<a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicate minerals">silicate minerals</a>, containing more of the lighter elements, such as silicon, oxygen, aluminium, sodium, and potassium).
</p><p>A common cause of the banding is the subjection of the <a href="/wiki/Protolith" title="Protolith">protolith</a> (the original rock material that undergoes metamorphism) to extreme shearing force, a sliding force similar to the pushing of the top of a deck of cards in one direction, and the bottom of the deck in the other direction.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshak_2009_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshak_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These forces stretch out the rock like a <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plastic</a>, and the original material is spread out into sheets.
</p><p>Some banding is formed from original rock material (protolith) that is subjected to extreme temperature and pressure and is composed of alternating layers of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> (lighter) and <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> (darker), which is metamorphosed into bands of <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshak_2009_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshak_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another cause of banding is "metamorphic differentiation", which separates different materials into different layers through chemical reactions, a process not fully understood.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshak_2009_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshak_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not all gneiss rocks have detectable banding. In kyanite gneiss, crystals of <a href="/wiki/Kyanite" title="Kyanite">kyanite</a> appear as random clumps in what is mainly a plagioclase (<a href="/wiki/Albite" title="Albite">albite</a>) <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(geology)" title="Matrix (geology)">matrix</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Augen_gneiss">Augen gneiss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Augen gneiss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg/220px-Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg/330px-Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg/440px-Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henderson_Augen_Gneiss.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Henderson augen gneiss</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Augen" title="Augen">Augen</a> gneiss, from the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Augen</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈaʊɡən]</a></span>, meaning "eyes", is a coarse-grained gneiss resulting from metamorphism of granite, which contains characteristic elliptic or <a href="/wiki/Lens_(geology)" title="Lens (geology)">lenticular</a> shear-bound feldspar <a href="/wiki/Porphyroclast" title="Porphyroclast">porphyroclasts</a>, normally <a href="/wiki/Microcline" title="Microcline">microcline</a>, within the layering of the quartz, biotite and magnetite bands.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Henderson_gneiss">Henderson gneiss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Henderson gneiss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Henderson gneiss is found in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, US, east of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brevard_Shear_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brevard Shear Zone (page does not exist)">Brevard Shear Zone</a>. It has deformed into two sequential forms. The second, more warped, form is associated with the Brevard Fault, and the first deformation results from displacement to the southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lewisian_gneiss">Lewisian gneiss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lewisian gneiss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg/220px-Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg/330px-Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg/440px-Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yttre_Ursholmen_Kontakt_Kosterdiabas_i_Nebulitisk-migmatitisk_sedimentgnejs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Contact between a dark-colored <a href="/wiki/Diabase" title="Diabase">diabase</a> dike (about 1100 million years old)<sup id="cite_ref-hageskov_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hageskov-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and light-colored <a href="/wiki/Migmatite" title="Migmatite">migmatitic</a> paragneiss in the <a href="/wiki/Kosterhavet_National_Park" title="Kosterhavet National Park">Kosterhavet National Park</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Koster_Islands" title="Koster Islands">Koster Islands</a> off the western coast of Sweden.</div></div></div>
<p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a> of Scotland have a bedrock formed from <a href="/wiki/Lewisian_complex" title="Lewisian complex">Lewisian gneiss</a>.  In addition to the Outer Hebrides, they form basement deposits on the Scottish mainland west of the <a href="/wiki/Moine_Thrust_Belt" title="Moine Thrust Belt">Moine Thrust</a> and on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Coll" title="Coll">Coll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiree" title="Tiree">Tiree</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> These rocks are largely igneous in origin, mixed with metamorphosed <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mica_schist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mica schist">mica schist</a> with later intrusions of basaltic <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dikes</a> and granite magma.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archean_and_Proterozoic_gneiss">Archean and Proterozoic gneiss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gneiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Archean and Proterozoic gneiss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
